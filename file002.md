Today was medical processing and some other interesting paperwork day.

The thin, uninsulated wall behind my bed is shared with a 24/7 cafe serving people entering or exiting the border, and perhaps those that work here in some of the nearby shops. There's apparently a TV a few feet from my head that loudly plays ...something, prettymuch all the time. Apparently, it's not really 24/7 because at some god awful hour of the night, the TV was shut off. I soon heard the creaking of what sounded like a bed spring above me and to the left. I imagine the proprietors sleep upstairs. I've not ventured in, but through the window I always see two very heavy women sitting in the corner. The sounds coraborrate this assumption. 

As I fell asleep, I kept waking to think someone was in the room with me, tapping the shelves or otherwise trying to get my attention. I got used to it quickly.  Gratefully, it is much quieter today. I can only guess why.

Anyhow, around 9 or 10, one of the sergeants (I presume) whom I've spoken to before, came in and explained to me and my room mate that we're going to separate battalions and I should prepare for a trip. He said I could bring my stuff, but I'd be returning here in the evening. I've been carrying these bags around all week and assume the locals knows it belongs to the army and would be safe enough. It is, however, all I own at this point. (Spoiler, it was fine.) Another soldier dropped off breakfast: sausages, something like cous cous, and beets. Man, they love beets here. They're starting to grow on me.

When the first guy came back, who speaks great english as well as spanish apparently, I was put into a tiny van which had a diesel and a manual transmission, and driven into town. A nice woman who spoke some english joined me. We were driven by an old soldier who spoke seemingly none.

The scenery on the ride was unique to my experience. A mile or two of the opposite flow of traffic had an endless line of vans parked on the shoulder, not moving. There was a police checkpoint that we could seemingly just blow through with a wave from the driver that they could not. Aside from that, there was an odd mix of dilapidated shops, nicely maintained homes, the occaisional religious shrine, agriculture fields, and unknown things under construction. No matter where I've been here, I see those same things. Every now and then there is a beautiful church, I assume, with painted domes looking like none I've seen elsewhere. 

The van was beat up, squeky, and the doors barely worked. No one wore a seat belt. The two in front chatted cheerfully the whole time, though I of course had no idea what they were saying. I found one or two times to chime in when it seemed appropriate. They asked me a few questions and were delighted to hear I was a veteran. Otherwise they left me alone. 

We arrived at a hospital the look of which would shock any American. It had to have been built by the Soviets. There's a coffee cafe right outside the front door. Going in through the mains, walking past the ash tray (EVERYONE smokes here), you're greeted with chaos. The building is long, narrow, split by one hallway, and several stories high. Four or five I think. There is no elevator. The stairs are very wide and worn down from years of use. The nice woman I mentioned earlier said "come," and we hiked up to almost the top floor. The hallways are just endless closed doors. She marched me to one after the other, passing the people waiting at each door, knocking, peeking her head in, and then ushering me in. I saw five or six doctors. Some of those experiences were hilarious. The dentist told me to open my mouth, took a quick look inside, said "GOOD," and then stamped my paper. We were off to the next one in less than two minutes. The dermatologist simply asked me if I have any skin problems. I said no, and she stamped the papers without really even looking at me. They did, however, give me a chest x-ray--I'm guessing more to see if my spine was intact more than anything, using a machine that looked 60 or more years old. The optomotrist said, "do you have any problems with your eyes?" I told her that I recently had an eye exam and everything was fine. Stamp. Moving on. The ear nose and through doctor actually looked at those things, wearing that classic, circular mirror on his head. He remarked how clean my ears were and sent me and my stamp along the way. The cardiologist was the most thorough. I laid down on a bed and she hooked up an equally ancient machine to my ankles, wrists, and chest, printed out something, stamped my paper and sent us along. Nice lady was with me the whole time. They all asked what state I was from. I'm not sure if it was interesting or proceedural. They took three blood samples and a urine sample. Maybe there was more, but nothing more than rubber stamps until we got to who I presume is the head doctor. 

None of these seemed to be on the same floor. Sometimes we'd knock and get told to go somewhere else, which we did, only to come back there again later. It was clean inside, but the floors were patchwork and everything was painted concrete with visible plumbing and electrical.

Head doctor lady looked over all the stamps, reviewed my cardiology print out for a moment (making some face when she did so) and approved all the rubber stamping. This actually took the longest.

They never took my blood pressure. 

Nice lady took me downstairs and outside. We met a different soldier and all had a smoke. Into a different, but similar van we went and drove to what looked like a house in a residental neighborhood aside from the fact that it had a sturdy, solid steel  gate with constantine wire atop it and the surrounding stone walls. We were spotted through a slide in an adjacent door and let through. I was led into a conference room and given a bunch of forms to fill out, an IQ test to take, and a CV to write. It took the better part of two hours. A high ranking enlisted woman (I dunno, she had a ton of stripes) brought me food! beets (of course) pasta noodles, some more of that strange protien balls, bread, chicken soup (no noodles, but there were bay leaves, and one of the chicken bits still had a bone in it), bread, and coffee. It was delicious. A few minutes after she served me, she came back in the room and fussed at me to eat and to take a break from the paperwork... so, of course I did that. 

After a while Nice Lady came back, had me sign my medical documents, gave me my passport back, showed me where the toilet was, and then we went outside for a smoke. 

A short while later, I was back in the van (without her for the first time) and back to the sleeping quarters by the border. Again we blew by MILES of traffic and with one word the soldier at the checkpoint waved us through. 

I walked to the store around the corner called "Alcohol and Tobacco" for both of those things. Bought some of each I've never heard of before and trundled back to my bed. Minutes after I sat down, a soldier came in and gave me more food. This time carrots, no beets sadly, sausage, and potatoes. He apparently didn't know what was in it, so after I sat down he said something in his native tounge while looking interested in the box. I guessed he wanted to know what it was. I opened it and he said probably one of the seven english words he knew, "Sweet!"

Another new soldier came in a while later to tell me that sadly I will have two or three days to relax here in my beautiful home before I can go to camp. I love their humor. He asked me if I cared where I got assigned, and I said I have no idea how to even answer that question. He made another joke about the dilapidated place and said, "Welcome to the Legion!" laughing loudly. 

Since I'm here alone for a few days, other than the food deliveries, I went poking around in the back. I found a lighter, some old magazines, and some booze. I left the magazines. 

Pretty cool day.
