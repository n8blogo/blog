I've arrived. It's Thursday. I started traveling Monday. A long flight into Europe and then a shorter one east. From there it got very interesting. As you can't fly directly into where I'm going, you know--war and all, you have to take trains or busses from somewhere nearby. Most of the locals who work behind counters are not very helpful to Americans who can't speak their language. I managed to communicate where I wanted to go and was just told "no trains." Well, there _aren't_ any trains directly from there to here, but I'm sure they could have booked all the connections for me, if they wanted to. None of the apps would take my card, though it worked fine in person. I walked for an hour to the train station. I should mention I was wearing a suit and dress shoes. I managed to find an automated ticketing machine that I could switch to (mostly) English. Using the map on my phone, I sorted out the connections myself. I bought the tickets one at a time at each station, using these machines; avoiding the ugly treatment.

It took forever. 

Anyway, I'm here now. The army converted a shabby old convenience store into a waiting area for the new recruits. It has five beds with some very sketchy looking sheets, a water closet--by definition only, and not much else. But hey, I can still walk around and use my stuff. Beats jail. Beats living in a van.

I was told we're going to wait here for a few more recruits to show up (a Mexican gentlemen just arrived at dusk) and then we'll be taken somewhere to do paperwork for temporary residency, medical exams, and stuff like that. 

I really should have slammed DuoLingo every chance I had before I got here. This language sounds very strange in my ears and many of them can't understand a word I'm saying either. I really wasn't sure I'd make it through all the customs and borders, which is probably why I didn't. Stupid. My Internet connection doesn't work in here, though it says I have signal, so I can't even start now.

It's funny, my roommate doesn't really speak any English, nor I Spanish, so even though we're here from the same side of the world, we aren't much good conversation for each other. At least I have a ton of audio books.

I could have brought more appropriate gear too, though I didn't really have the money for it. I have that plate carrier I found in the donation area from my Florida studio apartment, which is awesome. I'm sure they'll give me plates after boot.

I wonder how boot camp will go if we all can't understand each other. Anyway, they brought me some food: rice with gravy and carrots, beats, and some protein thing that sort of reminds me of a hush puppy. It's the most I've eaten in days, and I enjoyed it. I saved some from breakfast. There's a microwave on a table in the corner--no clue if it works.

Well, I wanted an interesting life, and this sure is interesting. Just a few more things to worry about. For one example, I almost always fail blood pressure tests when I'm nervous (which I am). I think I'm strong enough now, but I need to get stronger. Maybe I'm just tired.

Getting through processing, then through boot, and then all I have to worry about is not getting smoked.
