There's a Sihk that does... something around where I have been staying since Thursday. Every morning he opens up a tent and gives away chai or coffee for free. He wears a hi-vis vest that says something about emergency response. I thought he had nothing to do with the Legion, but it was him who came into the room this morning to tell me that they were ready for me to go. An old soldier I've seen around was waiting for me out there. I abandoned my dress suit, hiding it in one of the cabinets. He drove me to a little diner just down the road and got me a meal. I have a feeling that's where all my meals have been coming from, because it was basically the exact same stuff I've been eating since arriving. Just as I finished, another car pulled in next to his. He was dressed in all black, neck to toes. He drove us to Lviv to see the Tax Inspectorate at the Payers Service Center. I was issued a tax ID number, basically.

Looking around Lviv, you'd never know Ukraine was at war. It's a rather large, dense city full of people going about their lives. Shops are tucked in every corner. There's apparently a lot of business in selling flowers, for some reason. Anyway, we eventually arrived in Morshyn at the training base. It's not exactly what you might think of when you hear the term "base." It's basically two large buildings, walled and gated, nestled in town right among some appartment buildings. I filled out a bunch of paperwork, some with odd questions like what my phone's IMEI numbers are. My bags were searched, I was patted down, given some rules, and a tour, then let loose. I had some borschtsch, which was alright. Apparently, I'm not going to be really trained for a while. I'm the first one here for my training unit. There are some columbians in a group ahead of me. They gave me a room that has its own shower, though the shower head is missing so you just get a hose coming out of the wall. The hot water is... warmish.  

Some of the rules here are interesting. Since we're hiding in plain sight, no more than four people can be hanging around the front door at a time. You have to use a Ukrainian SIM, and disable roaming. You can't have locations services activated when you're on the base. They use the Signal app for everything. Video calls are not allowed. No pictures of anyone or anything on or nearby the base. No lights in rooms with windows, and the windows are all covered with taped up cardboard. Stuff like that. You can apparently ask permission to leave base to go shopping, but I'm not sure what that looks like yet, exactly. This definitely isn't boot camp. It's more like a training center with an air raid siren and a bomb shelter down below. This seems far more civilized than what I was expecting.
