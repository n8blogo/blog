This isn’t going to work.

A legal matter I thought I could handle remotely, it turns out, requires my physical presence. I’ll be traveling back to the States soon, in lieu of catching a bench warrant for non-appearance. That may not be a huge deal while I’m here, but it would be a problem in the future trying to travel back home as a wanted man, among other complications.

It’s disappointing after all this time, effort, and resources spent getting here, but it may be a blessing after all.

It’s been quite an experience here, even if briefly. I learned that none of this works how the recruiter told me it would. If you’ve heard “funny” stories about military recruiters in the US, no matter how deranged, they don’t hold a candle to the ILDU.

In the USA, when you enlist, you don’t even have to show up with a tooth brush. They will give you everything you need: a place to sleep, clothes to wear, a salary from day one, training, gear, etc. When I enlisted in the USAF, I didn’t even have a job reservation. I was given one commensurate with my testing scores before the end of basic training, and then sent off to the appropriate school to learn how to do that job. During school, you make a “wish list” of bases you’d like to be assigned to, which they consider, but the needs of the service come first, and you are sent off more or less directly from school to that base. If there is an interval between basic and school or school and first duty assignment, you still have a place to live and are still collecting salary.

The inverse of the above is true for the Legion. You have to bring everything you need, including uniforms (an oxymoron here, since none of the “uniforms” are therefore uniform), gear (think helmet, armor, boots, ear/eye protection, etc.), and everything else, including toilet paper. You’re sent to a processing facility which is more like a very patriotic jail that occasionally gets bombed (I spent last night underground in the bomb shelter listening to air raid sirens) with no pay or even basic provisions beyond a sleeping bag, food and water. (You have to return the sleeping bag.) During this time you have to interview for a position in a down range unit. If accepted, you have to wait until your background check clears and other government paperwork is completed by the government. During that interval, you must find lodging and provisions for yourself... somewhere. This can take a month or more. Assuming you get through that on your own, you must, at your own expense, then travel to said unit on the other side of the country. At that point, your salary of $500/month begins. If you do missions, you get paid more for the duration of that mission, and different missions pay different wages. Between missions, you can stay on base, which is usually a cot in a tent situation, unless you can afford your own apartment or something nearby. (Don't ask me how.)

Absolutely none of this was explained to me until this week.

I think, morally, volunteering to fight here is the right thing to do. I understand that this country has lost half or more of her soldiers in this conflict so far and they’re tired and broke, welcoming help. If I had a support system here, family or friends, I could see it working for me—legal issues back home notwithstanding.

The Russian empire should be stopped. They’ve killed several civilians here in just the short time I’ve been in the country. The Ukrainian people are kind, caring people—human beings—just like most of us. There are playgrounds with children laughing behind the schools just like everywhere else decent on Earth. If you want to help them, you can donate to: https://u24.gov.ua/
